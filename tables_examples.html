The algorithm for treatment of a patient with supratherapeutic INR depends on the INR level and presence of clinically serious bleeding.  Studies have shown that oral vitamin K is as effective as intravenous vitamin K in lowering the INR in 24 hours.  Since intravenous vitamin K has a risk of anaphylaxis, oral vitamin K is the preferred route.

Patients INR <4.5 and no clinical evidence of serious bleeding should have their warfarin held for 1 or 2 days and then restarted when the INR is in the therapeutic range.  Patients with INR 4.5-10 and no serious bleeding should have their warfarin held and be given a low dose (1-2.5 mg) of oral vitamin K if there is an increased risk of future bleeding (eg, history of prior bleed) (Choice A).  Patients with INR >10 and no serious bleeding should have their warfarin held and be given a higher dose (2.5-5 mg) of oral vitamin K.  Patients with serious bleeding, regardless of the INR, should have their warfarin held and receive intravenous vitamin K as well as prothrombin complex concentrate (PCC) (Choices C and E).

This patient has symptomatic hyperthyroidism, likely due to Graves disease (diffuse goiter, elevated free T4, suppressed TSH).  She also has hyperthyroid complications including rapid atrial fibrillation (irregularly irregular tachycardia with variable S1 intensity) and progressive bone loss with mild hypercalcemia.  Cardiovascular manifestations are common and can include other supraventricular arrhythmias (eg, atrial flutter, paroxysmal atrial tachycardia), hypertension, coronary vasospasm, myocardial ischemia, and congestive heart failure.

Patients being considered for noncardiac surgery should undergo preoperative cardiovascular risk assessment with a validated prediction tool that incorporates both procedure- and patient-specific variables.  Although newer models (eg, American College of Surgeons surgical risk calculator) are available, the Revised Cardiac Risk Index (RCRI) remains relevant due to its prolonged validation and simplicity (ie, 1 point each for 6 possible variables).

Based on the RCRI, this patient's only patient-specific risk factor is a history of congestive heart failure (which is currently compensated); and she is undergoing a procedure with a low procedure-specific risk (eg, breast surgery).  Therefore, given her RCRI score (1), she has a 1% estimated risk of perioperative major cardiac complication (eg, myocardial infarction, cardiac arrest, cardiac death).  This is the recommended threshold for undergoing surgery without further testing.

When estimated risk is >1% (eg, RCRI >1), further preoperative evaluation requires assessment of cardiac functional capacity.  The ability to perform activities requiring ≥4 metabolic equivalents (METs) determines the need for further testing.

  • Patients with increased risk (>1%) who can perform ≥4 METs (eg, climb a flight of stairs) can also proceed to surgery without further evaluation.

  • Patients with increased risk (>1%) and unknown or poor (<4 METs) functional status require further consideration.  However, pharmacologic myocardial perfusion imaging is recommended only if cardiac intervention (if necessary) will impact surgical/perioperative management (eg, postponing elective surgery for revascularization followed by 6 months of antiplatelet therapy).  Pharmacologic-based testing is recommended over exercise-based testing (eg, treadmill with echocardiography) because these patients are less likely to achieve an exercise workload adequate for diagnosis (Choices D and E).

Generally, most patients do not require extensive preoperative cardiac testing except for high-risk procedures in those with significant cardiac conditions.

(Choice C)  Preoperative resting echocardiogram is indicated for patients with heart failure or valvular dysfunction when clinical evaluation reveals a change in clinical status.  This patient's compensated heart failure and mild, asymptomatic mitral regurgitation (eg, no dyspnea, no jugular venous distension) do not require further testing.

Educational objective:
Patients undergoing noncardiac surgery should be assessed for perioperative cardiac risk based on procedural- and patient-specific risk factors.  Low-risk (≤1%) patients as well as those at increased risk (>1%) with adequate functional capacity (≥4 metabolic equivalents) can typically undergo surgery without further testing.

This patient's postmenopausal bleeding is likely due to pelvic organ prolapse (POP).  POP occurs as a result of damage to or atrophy of the ligaments, muscles, and nerves that support the pelvic organs.  This damage leads to herniation of the uterus (apical prolapse), bladder (cystocele), or rectum (rectocele) through the vagina, resulting in vaginal pressure and a bulging mass at the introitus.  Although POP can be asymptomatic, patients often have associated urinary (eg, incontinence, retention), defecatory (eg, constipation), or sexual dysfunction (eg, dyspareunia).  Patients with severe, prolonged prolapse can develop vaginal or cervical erosions if the cervix and vaginal apex protrude through the introitus, rub on clothing, and become inflamed and denuded.  These erosions often cause abnormal vaginal bleeding (eg, postcoital, postmenopausal). 

Risk factors for POP include obesity, increasing age, high parity, and history of operative vaginal delivery.  Management options for POP include a pessary and surgical correction, which are equally efficacious.  In addition, administration of vaginal estrogen is indicated to facilitate healing of cervicovaginal erosions.

In cases of acute diverticulitis, if symptoms do not improve after 2-3 days of appropriate treatment (eg, analgesic medication, liquid diet, +/- oral antibiotics), patients should be hospitalized and undergo repeat CT scan of the abdomen and pelvis to evaluate for abscess or other complications such as obstruction or perforation.

Colonic abscess is the most common complication of acute diverticulitis, occurring in 15%-55% of cases.  Symptoms may resemble those of uncomplicated diverticulitis but often do not improve with conservative management.  Diverticular abscess is usually confirmed on repeat abdominopelvic CT scan as a localized fluid collection.  Most abscesses require percutaneous drainage and intravenous antibiotics followed by consideration for elective partial colectomy several weeks later.  If the CT scan shows no abscess or other complications, intravenous antibiotics should be given until the patient shows clinical improvement or an alternate explanation is determined.

(Choice B)  Colonoscopy should be performed in most patients with diverticulitis 6-8 weeks after the complete resolution of symptoms to rule out colon cancer.  Colonoscopy is not recommended in acute management due to the risk for perforation.

Determining the cause of dyspnea during hemodialysis can be challenging, in part because large, physiologic shifts (eg, fluid shifts) are occurring, but also because hemodialysis patients often have multiple comorbidities (eg, cardiovascular disease) that can manifest with dyspnea.  Dyspnea prior to initiation of hemodialysis often results from volume overload and is common.  However, dyspnea that begins after initiation of hemodialysis may reflect another medical condition (eg, acute coronary syndrome, anaphylaxis) that warrants further workup.

This patient developed dyspnea with wheezing, hypotension, and flushing shortly after intravenous (IV) iron infusion.  This is concerning for anaphylaxis, which may be diagnosed when cutaneous symptoms (eg, flushing) and either hypotension or respiratory distress (this patient has both) occur after exposure to a possible allergen (eg, IV iron).  Allergic symptoms in ≥2 systems (eg, skin, respiratory, cardiovascular) after possible allergen exposure is another diagnostic criterion for anaphylaxis.

IV iron is a recognized allergen.  Although anaphylaxis is rare, IV iron infusions are common as the preferred treatment for anemia in hemodialysis patients (~70% of patients).  Anaphylaxis may also be triggered by other medications (eg, antibiotics) administered during hemodialysis or by the dialysate itself.  Early recognition and treatment with epinephrine can be lifesaving.

(Choice A)  Hemodialysis patients have an increased incidence of coronary artery disease and can develop acute myocardial infarction (AMI), causing chest tightness and dyspnea.  However, AMI causing hypotension (eg, cardiogenic shock) would likely lead to pale skin (vs flushing) due to hypoperfusion and crackles (vs wheezing) due to acute pulmonary edema.  This patient's lack of chest pain and his symptom onset after IV iron infusion make anaphylaxis more likely.

(Choice B)  Venous air embolism can cause dyspnea and hypotension, particularly if the air embolus travels to pulmonary circulation.  However, flushing and wheezing would be unexpected.

(Choice D)  Dialysis patients can have small pericardial effusions (eg, slightly muffled heart sounds) due to volume overload.  Large uremic effusions can lead to cardiac tamponade presenting with symptoms of cardiogenic shock (eg, dyspnea, hypotension) and impaired venous return (eg, peripheral edema).  However, distended neck veins are also frequently present, and skin flushing and wheezing are not expected.

(Choice E)  Pulmonary embolism can cause dyspnea, chest tightness, hypotension, and tachycardia; however, flushing and bilateral wheezing are more suggestive of anaphylaxis.

Educational objective:
Although anaphylaxis can be difficult to recognize in hemodialysis patients due to large fluid shifts and confounding patient comorbidities, it should be considered as a potential cause of dyspnea in patients receiving intravenous iron infusions or other medications during hemodialysis.

Active error

Latent error

  • Proximal cause
  • Involves actions of frontline personnel
  • Occurs at interface of provider with patient or system
  • "Sharp end" of the scalpel, tip of the iceberg
  • Distal cause
  • Involves organizational vulnerabilities that influence actions of frontline personnel

This scenario (based on a real case) involves errors related to mislabeling, a risk factor for potentially catastrophic transfusion errors.  In complex systems (eg, operating room, hospital), safety incidents involve both active errors, describing incorrect, proximal actions of front-line personnel (eg, human error); and latent errors, describing system-level, distal failures.

As seen in this case, blood transfusion protocols involve multistep safety protocols such as confirmatory type and cross-matching of patient blood prior to release of products.  Two active errors allowed incorrectly labeled blood products to reach the operating room:

  • Personnel at the blood bank incorrectly labeled an empty tube and sent it to the anesthesiologist for collection.
  • The anesthesiologist did not double-check the vial before drawing blood into the mislabeled tube and returning it to the blood bank.

Safety incidents are rarely isolated results of incorrect individual action; rather, they are enabled by failures in latent, systemic issues or distal causes.  Examples of potential latent factors underlying these incidents include:

  • inadequate staffing of the blood bank, which could have increased workload and fatigue, leading to label confusion (Choice B).
  • organizational policies allowing prelabeling (eg, labeling empty collection tube before drawing blood) rather than bedside labeling (eg, labeling tube after blood is drawn, in the patient's presence).  Bedside labeling could have greatly reduced mislabeling risk in this case (Choice C).
  • patient complexity, a distal cause that can increase provider distractibility and pressure (eg, increasing risk of not double-checking) (Choice D).

(Choice E)  Although inadequate communication by the surgeon is an example of active error, it is less relevant to this case, which involved interactions among the anesthesiologist, patient, and blood bank.

Educational objective:
Root causes of medical errors include latent errors, describing system-level safety vulnerabilities, and active errors, describing frontline personnel's actions.

Atrial fibrillation is a common postoperative complication of cardiac surgery.  Patients with risk factors for underlying substrate that encourages atrial fibrillation (eg, advanced age, hypertension, left ventricular dysfunction) are at greatest risk of developing postoperative atrial fibrillation (POAF).  Cardiac surgery likely provides transient encouragement of atrial fibrillation via a further increase in underlying substrate (eg, surgery-related atrial myocardial inflammation) and an increased rate of triggers resulting from a surgery-related increase in sympathetic tone.

Most patients with no history of atrial fibrillation who develop POAF following cardiac surgery will spontaneously convert to sinus rhythm within a few days.  However, such conversion to sinus rhythm does not signify resolution of atrial fibrillation and freedom from its associated complications.  The development of POAF likely indicates substantial underlying substrate, and many patients will have recurrent episodes of atrial fibrillation (ie, paroxysmal disease) both during hospitalization and following discharge, placing them at risk for complications, including embolic stroke and heart failure (Choice E).  POAF is also associated with increased long-term mortality.

(Choice A)  Myocardial reperfusion commonly precipitates an accelerated idioventricular rhythm, which appears similar on ECG to ventricular tachycardia but with a ventricular rate <100/min.  Reperfusion is not a precipitant of atrial fibrillation.

(Choice B)  Acute ischemia is not a common or direct trigger of atrial fibrillation.  Myocardial infarction (MI) can sometimes precipitate atrial fibrillation via increased sympathetic tone or abrupt onset left ventricular failure with atrial stretching; however, MI is unlikely in this patient in the absence of chest pain.

(Choice C)  Spontaneous conversion to sinus rhythm is typical of POAF, and the risk of recurrence is high.  Cardioversion, either pharmacologic or electrical, is not indicated and is unlikely to be successful because affected patients usually have significant underlying substrate for atrial fibrillation, making sustained maintenance of sinus rhythm difficult.

Educational objective:
Postoperative atrial fibrillation (POAF) is common following cardiac surgery.  Most patients spontaneously convert to sinus rhythm within a few days; however, the occurrence of POAF likely indicates significant underlying substrate for atrial fibrillation with ongoing risk for recurrence and complications (eg, stroke, heart failure).
